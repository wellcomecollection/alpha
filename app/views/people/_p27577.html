James Gillray

James Gillray (13 August 1756 or 1757 – 1 June 1815) was an English caricaturist and printmaker famous for his etched political and social satires, mainly published between 1792 and 1810. Wikipedia

Many of Gillray’s prints depicted various forms of medical conditions and interventions, often lampooning quack doctors and painful ‘cures’. That's largely why there are lots of his works at Wellcome.

A Selection of Gillray's Works

Henry Wellcome actively collected satires as rich iconographic sources of social comment on hot topics of their time - the medical profession, for example. There is scarce provenance information on many of them but it is likely that he acquired in groups at auction not individually as they might have been sold originally.

Medical Men and Quacks

The medical industry was, shall we say, an emerging industry. Various so-called physicians, apothecaries, and surgeons were licensed in all kinds of colourful ways, and unrepentently practiced on any citizens who could afford them.

It was a seller's market, with the catchiest advertisements snaring the sick and the desperate, only to subject them to unbelievable "treatments".

"Metallic Tractors" were a type of quack treatment invented (and patented) by Elisha Perkins. They used the body's own electricity to expel disease with a metal rod. Tractors cured many inflammatory diseases, including gout, or so its inventor Elisha Perkins have you believe...

Vaccination

Around 1798, Edward Jenner made the discovery that taking the pus from a cow with cow pox (yes, a cow), and injecting that pus into a human, actually vaccinated the human against smallpox. Well, no wonder everyone thought he was a immoral lunatic.

Look, you can see Jenner in the print below, injecting pus into a skeptical woman surround by cow-humans.

Medicine and Satire

An explosion in cheap printing techniques and enviable of freedom of expression gave caricaturists such as Gillray, Rowlandson and Williams a ready market for the work. Gillray was perhaps the most viscious of the satirists, unafraid to show even the King on his special, private "throne".

Printmaking and Copies

Wellcome Library has lots of prints and versions of many of the Gillray sketches. They’re grouped together physically in the stores, but are catalogued separately, with no connection made between them.

Here we’ve arranged them together again so you can compare them to each other. Note that some versions are mirrored, or hand-coloured differently.

Taking PHYSICK

This selection depicts a patient taking Physick, an old term for a laxative (hence the presence of the toilet in the background in some versions). The last one probably isn't even by Gillray, but is a copy/re-interpretation done by another artist.

The Gout

This one depicts a metaphorical monster sinking his teeth into a swollen foot, representing the extreme pain of the illness gout.

Gentle Emetic

Emetic means vomiting, so given the pained expression of this patient’s expression, and the large bowl in front of him, gentle here might be somewhat satirical.

Breathing a vein

This gruesome procedure, known as bloodletting, was quite common – and likely had no health benefits whatsover.

Easing the Toothach [sic]

Toothache is clearly not a modern phenomenon. It’s not quite clear what the dentists are actually doing here, but looks like it hurts.

Some of these prints also seem to have non-Gillray names on them, so it's not clear if they're copies or if other artists might have contributed as engravers. The last version seems to have been used in an actual advert for an dentist! Who's laughing now?

Gillray's Contemporaries and Subjects

Gillray often satirised monarchs, world leaders and politicians, even personifying countries, like representing Britain as a character as "John Bull". He was also supported by a network of printers and publishers in London at the time.

 
Hannah Humphrey

1745-1818
Hannah Humprey operated a print shop in London, exclusively publishing Gillray’s works from 1791 onward.

Thomas Rowlandson

1756-1827
Thomas Rowlandson was Gillray’s contemporary and friend. His works depicted English social life more light-heartedly.

George III

1738-1820
King. Went a bit mad. Possibly had porpyhria.

Charles James Fox

1749-1806
Statesman and a pro-revolutionary (both French and American), Fox was the subject of many of Gillray’s prints.

Henry Addington, Viscount Sidmouth

1757-1844
British Statesman. Prime Minister from 1801-1804.

Napoleon I

1769-1821

1759-1806
Politician. The Younger. Prime Minister at 24, during the reign of George III.

Elisha Perkins

American doctor. Invented the metallic tractor and antiseptic medicine.

 
Charles Williams

Broader Contemporary Themes

Gillray was operating at the inception of an important time in medical history, and used many broader themes in his work. If you'd like to explore the collection further, please do!

History of Medicine

Much of contemporary medical practice involved trying to get things out of the body, making people vomit, helping them pass various things out the other end, or bloodletting. All this purging was about balancing the humours - a medical theory going back to the Ancient Greeks.

Gillray's lifetime was also the Age of Enlightenment, and natural philosophy (later called Science) was definitely influencing medicine. Natural philosophy was also a popular topic for Gillray, and he made four prints about it.

The Metallic Tractor

Like many quack cures the metallic tractor was based on a real scientific discovery. In the 1780s Luigi Galvani made the legs of a dissected frog twitch with an electrical current. He called this phenomenon animal electricity and sparked the imagination of a lot of quacks, but also Mary Shelley, the author of Frankenstein.

Gout

Gout was a hot topic around 1771, as you can see from the publishing history graph. Symptoms include acute pain in swollen joints. Today, gout is known to be caused by excess of uric acid in the blood, but in its long history it was associated with living too well. It's called podagora when you get it in your big toe.

You might like to skim Observations on the nature and cure of gout; on nodes of the joints; and on the influence of certain articles of diet, in gout, rheumatism, and gravel by James Parkinson. It's a cracking read.
