Gillray at the Wellcome Library

Many of Gillray’s prints depicted various medical conditions and interventions, often lampooning quack doctors and painful ‘cures’. That's largely why there are lots of his works at Wellcome. Henry Wellcome actively collected satires as rich iconographic sources of social comment. There is scarce provenance information on many of them but it is likely that he acquired them in groups at auction.

Medical Men and Quacks

The medical trade was, shall we say, an emerging industry. Various physicians, apothecaries, and surgeons were licensed in all kinds of colourful ways - or not at all - and unrepentently practiced on any citizens who could afford them.

It was a medical marketplace, with the catchiest advertisements snaring the sick and the desperate, often subjecting them to unbelievable "treatments".

"Metallic Tractors" were a type of quack treatment invented (and patented) by Elisha Perkins. They used the body's own electricity to expel disease with a metal rod. Tractors cured many inflammatory diseases, including gout, or so its inventor Elisha Perkins have you believe...

Vaccination

Around 1798, Edward Jenner made the discovery that taking the pus from a cow with cow pox (yes, a cow), and injecting that pus into a human, actually protected the human against the more deadly smallpox. But no wonder people were suspicious that this was just another quack cure.

Look, you can see Jenner in the print below, injecting cow pox into an anxious woman surround by cow-humans.

Edward Jenner vaccinating patients in the Smallpox and Inoculation Hospital at St. Pancras: the patients develop features of cows. Coloured etching by J. Gillray, 1802.

Medicine and Satire

An explosion in cheap printing techniques and enviable freedom of expression in 18th century Britain gave caricaturists such as Gillray, Rowlandson and Williams a ready market for their work. Gillray was perhaps the most viscious of the satirists, unafraid to show even the King on his special, private "throne".

Charles James Fox, dangerously ill, visited by an entourage of interested factions:representing the social and ministerial conflict surrounding him

Printmaking and Copies

The Wellcome Library has many versions of the same Gillray print. All the versions are grouped together physically in the stores, but are catalogued as individual items online.

Here we’ve arranged them together again so you can compare them to each other. Some of the prints date from after Gilray's death, clearly the publishers continued to issue new prints from the existing engraving plates.

Taking PHYSICK

In some cases the prints are mirror images of each other. This is where a later engraver has made a new plate by tracing the image from an earlier print.

This set depicts a patient taking a physick (medicine), perhaps a laxative (hence the presence of the toilet in the background in some versions). The last one probably isn't even by Gillray, but is a copy/re-interpretation done by another artist.

The Gout

This group depicts a devilish creature sinking his teeth into a swollen foot, representing the extreme pain of the illness gout. The sequence of prints shows different stages of printing, from line drawing to the addition of colour.

Gentle Emetic

Emetics make you vomit, so given the pained expression of this patient’s expression, and the large bowl in front of him, gentle here is somewhat satirical.

Breathing a vein

Breathing a vein was a popular term for bloodletting. It was a common procedure for all manor of illness.

Easing the Toothach [sic]

It’s not quite clear what the dentists are actually doing here, but looks like it hurts. The last version 'borrows' Gillray's print in an actual advert for a dentist!
